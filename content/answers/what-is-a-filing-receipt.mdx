A filing receipt is a confirmation record generated when a company filing — such as [dormant accounts](/answers/what-are-dormant-company-accounts) or a [CT600](/answers/what-is-a-ct600) — is accepted by Companies House or HMRC. It serves as proof that the filing was submitted and accepted for a specific accounting period.

## What it contains

A typical filing receipt includes:

- **Company name and company registration number (CRN)** — identifying which company the filing relates to
- **Filing type** — whether it was dormant accounts (Companies House) or a CT600 (HMRC)
- **Accounting period** — the start and end dates covered by the filing
- **Submission reference** — a unique identifier assigned to the submission
- **IRmark** — a digital fingerprint of the document content, used by HMRC and Companies House to verify the filing has not been altered
- **Acceptance date** — when the filing authority confirmed it was accepted

## Why you might need one

Most of the time, filing receipts sit in a folder and are never looked at again. But they become important when:

- **HMRC sends a penalty notice** claiming a CT600 was not filed — the receipt with its submission reference and IRmark is your evidence that it was
- **A co-director or shareholder** wants confirmation that company obligations are being met
- **An accountant, solicitor, or mortgage broker** asks for proof the company is compliant
- **You are selling or transferring the company** and the buyer wants to verify the filing history

It is always easier to save a receipt at the time of filing than to reconstruct proof months later.
